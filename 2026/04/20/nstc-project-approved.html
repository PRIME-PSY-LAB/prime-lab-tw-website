NSTC Research Project Approved

yen-ju chen April 20, 2026 April 26, 2026
news grant research object-recognition

I am pleased to share that my NSTC research project was approved on April 20, 2026.

The project is titled “From Perceptual Structure to Semantic Space: A Human-centered Computational Framework for Object Recognition”. Its central goal is to understand how humans recognize objects in dynamic and noisy visual environments, and to use this understanding to build computational models that are better aligned with human perception.

In everyday vision, object recognition feels effortless. We can identify objects despite changes in viewpoint, lighting, motion, occlusion, and noise. However, this ability depends on a complex sequence of computations: the visual system must organize unstable image signals into meaningful perceptual units, connect those units to object structure, and eventually map them onto conceptual knowledge.

Current deep learning models achieve impressive performance in object classification, but they often rely heavily on static image features. They do not fully capture how humans use temporal information, structured representations, and conceptual context.

This project will approach the problem from three connected directions.

First, we will use psychophysical experiments to examine how humans use temporal correlations to form perceptual units. This line of work asks whether dynamic visual signals can reveal the computational principles behind perceptual organization.

Second, we will develop new methods for measuring object and scene concepts. By combining behavioral experiments with generative AI tools, the project aims to describe conceptual spaces in a more controllable and quantitative way.

Third, we will build and evaluate computational models of object recognition. Rather than focusing only on classification accuracy, the goal is to test whether models can reproduce human-like patterns of perception, including sensitivity to spatiotemporal structure, robustness under noisy conditions, and concept-dependent interference during recognition.

Over the next three years, this project will integrate visual psychophysics, computational modeling, and artificial intelligence to study how stable object knowledge emerges from dynamic visual input. I hope this work will contribute not only to vision science, but also to the development of more interpretable and human-centered artificial vision systems.

I am grateful for the support from NSTC and excited to begin this next stage of research at PRIME Lab.
