Overview

This project examined how positive emotion, arousal, and approach motivation jointly shape human perceived time. While many studies report that high-arousal emotional events “feel longer,” results for positive emotion are surprisingly inconsistent. Some experiments find that high-arousal positive pictures lengthen perceived time, others find no effect, and some even show shorter perceived durations. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

The working hypothesis was that these divergent findings are not noise, but reflect the influence of approach motivation. High approach motivation (e.g., wanting to obtain a reward) tends to narrow attention and make time feel shorter, whereas high arousal tends to speed up the internal clock and make time feel longer.
This project tested how these forces interact, using the Scalar Expectancy Theory (SET) / pacemaker–accumulator model as the theoretical framework.

Methods

Participants

Across three experiments, undergraduate students from Soochow University (approximately 30–40 participants per experiment) completed a computerized time-estimation task for course credit or a small monetary reward. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

Stimuli

  • Pictures were selected from the International Affective Picture System (IAPS).
  • Only positive-valence images were used, split into:
    • Low-arousal positive pictures
    • High-arousal positive pictures
  • Valence was matched across the two sets, whereas arousal ratings differed reliably (high-arousal > low-arousal). :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

Task and design

Participants viewed pictures for one of eight durations (400–1800 ms) and produced verbal estimates of the perceived duration in milliseconds.
The core factors were:

  • Arousal: high vs. low (within-subject)
  • Approach motivation: high vs. low (between-subject)
  • Presentation order (between-subject, varied across experiments):
    • Experiment 1: fully random mixing of high- and low-arousal pictures
    • Experiment 2: low → high arousal in blocked order
    • Experiment 3: high → low arousal in blocked order

Approach motivation was manipulated by reward contingency:

  • High-approach condition: participants were told they could obtain some of the picture stimuli (or related postcards) as a reward after the experiment.
  • Low-approach condition: no such expectation was created, though all participants later received a small gift so that ethical treatment was identical. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

For each participant and condition, a linear regression of estimated time on physical time was fitted. Within the SET framework:

  • The slope indexes the pacemaker rate.
  • The intercept reflects the effective switch / attentional gate, with more negative intercepts indicating more pulse loss.

Key results

Experiment 1 – Random presentation

  • With randomly interleaved high- and low-arousal pictures, high-arousal images were generally judged as lasting longer than low-arousal ones, consistent with a basic arousal effect.
  • Approach motivation did not produce a clear main effect on perceived duration.
  • In SET terms, arousal modestly affected the intercept, but there was no robust evidence that pacemaker rate differed across conditions. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

Interpretation: Random mixing likely allowed high-arousal trials to “spill over” onto neighboring low-arousal trials through slow emotional recovery, masking the role of approach motivation.

Experiment 2 – Blocked order, low → high arousal

  • When all low-arousal pictures were presented first and high-arousal pictures later, a clear interaction emerged:
    • In the low-approach group, high-arousal pictures produced longer perceived durations than low-arousal pictures.
    • In the high-approach group, this arousal advantage disappeared: high- and low-arousal pictures were perceived with similar durations.
  • On average, high approach motivation shortened perceived time relative to low approach motivation.
  • Linear-model analysis showed:
    • Under high approach, the pacemaker slope was reduced, especially for high-arousal pictures.
    • For low approach, high arousal increased perceived time mainly by raising the intercept, consistent with more effective pulse accumulation. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

Interpretation: When arousal is cleanly separated in time, approach motivation counteracts the lengthening effect of arousal, largely by slowing the effective pacemaker rate or increasing pulse loss.

Experiment 3 – Blocked order, high → low arousal

  • With the opposite order (high → low arousal), the pattern changed again: overall, high approach motivation led to longer perceived durations.
  • The main effect of arousal was weaker and less stable, but time estimates still increased with physical duration.
  • Analysis suggested that in this schedule, approach motivation mainly influenced the slope, with high-approach participants showing a generally faster internal clock across both arousal levels. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}

Interpretation: Presenting high-arousal pictures first may recruit emotion regulation or attentional strategies that alter how subsequent trials are processed. The contrast between Experiments 2 and 3 highlights the importance of temporal context and emotional recovery when interpreting arousal and motivation effects on time perception.

Integrated conclusions

  1. Positive arousal alone tends to lengthen perceived time, but this effect is modulated by approach motivation and by how trials are ordered.
  2. High approach motivation can:
    • Shorten perceived time (especially when low-arousal trials are encountered first), or
    • Under some temporal contexts, increase the pacemaker rate and lengthen time.
  3. Within the Scalar Expectancy Theory framework:
    • Arousal primarily impacts the pacemaker and sometimes the gate.
    • Approach motivation can operate on both components: reducing pacemaker speed in high-arousal contexts and increasing pulse loss via the attentional gate when arousal is low. :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}
  4. These findings help explain why positive-emotion studies show inconsistent arousal effects: different experiments implicitly induce different levels of approach motivation and use different presentation schedules.

Methods and equipment

Across experiments, the lab used:

  • A desktop computer with precise millisecond timing for stimulus presentation.
  • Standard LCD displays and controlled viewing distance in a quiet testing room.
  • Keyboard-based numeric input for verbal duration estimates.
  • Custom experimental scripts that randomized picture–duration pairings within each block.

This project illustrates how psychophysical timing paradigms and emotion manipulations can be combined to dissect cognitive mechanisms of time perception.
