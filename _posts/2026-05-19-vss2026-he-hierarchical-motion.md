On May 19, 2026, my junior colleague from Kyoto University, Ziwei He, presented our poster at the Vision Sciences Society annual meeting. The project is titled **"Multi-Layered Hierarchical Motion: Ancestors Matter, Descendants Do Not"**, with Ziwei He, Yen-Ju Chen, Yung-Hao Yang, and Shin&rsquo;ya Nishida as authors.

[Download the VSS 2026 poster]({{ "/assets/posters/vss2026-he-hierarchical-motion-poster.pdf" | relative_url }})

![Preview of the VSS 2026 poster]({{ "/images/blog/vss2026-he-hierarchical-motion/poster-preview.png" | relative_url }})

The study asks a deceptively simple question: when a visual scene contains several nested moving layers, which parts of the hierarchy actually shape perceived relative motion? In everyday perception, motion is rarely a single vector. A walking person translates through space, limbs swing relative to the body, and local elements can move within larger moving structures. Human vision must therefore decide not only what is moving, but also which object should serve as the reference frame for that motion.

The poster tested this problem using a vector matching paradigm. Participants saw stimuli with multiple hierarchical levels, such as a background, a larger object, and smaller nested objects. Before each trial, they were told which layer was the target and which layer was the reference. After viewing the motion, they reproduced the perceived motion of the target relative to the reference.

The key result is captured in the poster title. Motion information from higher-level reference objects, the "ancestors" of the target in the hierarchy, strongly shaped perception. By contrast, lower-level task-irrelevant components, the "descendants," had little influence. In other words, the visual system can decompose a complex motion hierarchy, but its precision is constrained by hierarchical distance and by which object is treated as the reference frame.

This result is important because it moves the discussion of induced motion beyond a single reference object. It suggests that relative motion perception is not just a local comparison between two moving parts, but a structured inference over a hierarchy. For visual science, that matters: it gives us a more precise way to ask how the visual system organizes nested motion signals into a stable perceptual scene.

I am glad to see this project represented at VSS, and especially glad that Ziwei could share it with the community in poster form. Poster sessions are often where a study becomes sharper: people point to the figure, ask where the boundary case lies, and force the theory to become more explicit.
